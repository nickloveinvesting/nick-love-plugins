# Voice Analysis Reference

## Voice DNA Analysis Process

### Step 1: Collect Content Samples

Gather 5-10 pieces of existing content:
- Blog posts
- Social media posts
- Emails
- Website copy
- Video transcripts
- Podcast transcripts

### Step 2: Analyze Voice Elements

Tone Spectrum:
- Formal to Casual
- Serious to Playful
- Reserved to Bold
- Professional to Friendly

Sentence Structure:
- Average length
- Variation patterns
- Use of fragments
- Question frequency

Vocabulary:
- Industry jargon (and when used)
- Colloquialisms
- Power words
- Avoided words

Personality Markers:
- Use of humor
- Personal anecdotes
- Controversial takes
- Level of directness

### Step 3: Create Voice DNA Profile

Tone:
- Primary: confident
- Secondary: approachable
- Avoid: corporate, stiff

Formality: casual professional

Sentence patterns:
- Average length: 12-18 words
- Uses fragments: true
- Question frequency: moderate

Vocabulary:
- Uses jargon: sparingly, always explained
- Colloquialisms: here is the thing, real talk
- Power words: actually, specific, proven
- Avoided words: leverage, synergy, optimize

Personality:
- Humor: occasional dry wit
- Stories: personal anecdotes to illustrate points
- Opinions: willing to take stands
- Directness: very direct, does not hedge

Formatting:
- Paragraph length: 2-4 sentences max
- Uses bullets: true
- Uses bold: for emphasis, sparingly
- Emoji usage: minimal, strategic

CTA style: soft ask, value-first

## Voice Types and Examples

### The Expert
- Confident assertions
- Data-driven claims
- Minimal hedging
- Authoritative tone

Example:
Let me be direct: most landing pages fail because of one simple mistake.
After analyzing 500+ pages, the pattern is clear.
Here is exactly what you need to fix.

### The Friend
- Conversational
- Empathetic
- Uses we and us
- Shares struggles

Example:
Look, I get it. Marketing is overwhelming.
I have been there - staring at a blank page, wondering 
what the hell to write.
Here is what finally worked for me...

### The Teacher
- Step-by-step
- Explanatory
- Patient tone
- Anticipates questions

Example:
Let me break this down.
First, understand that headlines have one job: stop the scroll.
That is it. Everything else comes second.
Here is how to nail it...

### The Provocateur
- Bold claims
- Contrarian views
- Challenges assumptions
- High energy

Example:
Everything you have been told about copywriting is wrong.
Seriously. Throw out the playbook.
Here is what actually works in 2024...

### The Storyteller
- Narrative structure
- Emotional hooks
- Character-driven
- Scene-setting

Example:
Three years ago, I sent an email that changed everything.
It was 3am. I had been up all night trying to crack the code.
Then I tried something different...

## Voice Consistency Checklist

Before publishing, verify:

- Does this sound like [Brand/Person] would say it?
- Would I be surprised to see this from them?
- Is the formality level consistent?
- Are the signature phrases present naturally?
- Is the energy level right?
- Would they use these specific words?
- Is the sentence rhythm similar?
- Does the opening match their style?

## Adapting Voice for Different Contexts

### Same Voice, Different Contexts

Homepage: Core voice, aspirational, polished
Blog: More casual, educational, longer
Email: Personal, conversational, direct
Social: Punchy, engaging, personality-forward
Sales Page: Persuasive, benefit-focused, proof-heavy
Error Messages: Helpful, reassuring, brief

### Voice Consistency Rules

1. Core traits stay constant across all channels
2. Intensity varies by context (sales page vs error message)
3. Length varies but rhythm stays similar
4. Personality shows even in functional copy

## Common Voice Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Corporate Speak
Bad: We leverage innovative solutions to optimize your workflow.
Good: We help you get more done in less time.

### Mistake 2: Inconsistent Formality
Bad: Hey there! Our enterprise-grade SaaS solution...
Good: Hey there! Our software helps teams ship faster...

### Mistake 3: Missing Personality
Bad: Our product has many features.
Good: Here is what you can actually do with this thing...

### Mistake 4: Over-explaining
Bad: This, as you probably know, is important because...
Good: This matters because...

### Mistake 5: Hedging
Bad: This might possibly help you perhaps improve...
Good: This helps you improve...

## Quality Check: The Founder Test

After writing any piece of content:

Would [Brand Owner/Founder] actually publish this 
under their name without significant edits?

- If No then Iterate
- If Maybe then Iterate
- If Yes then Move forward

### Red Flags That Fail The Test
- Generic opening paragraph
- No specific data or examples
- Could have been written by anyone
- Obvious AI patterns (In conclusion, It is important to note)
- No personality or voice
- Safe takes only
- Missing signature phrases
- Wrong energy level
- Too formal or too casual for brand
